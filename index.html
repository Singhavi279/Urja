About Us

#What is GirlUp?
GirlUp was founded by the United Nations in 2010, as an initiative to help support UN agencies that focus on adolescent girls. GirlUp Clubs are formed by groups of dedicated youth who have joined together to take action and change lives, empowering both people in their own communities and adolescent girls in developing countries to rise up simultaneously.

#What is GirlUp Urja and its focus?
GirlUp Urja aims to throw light on two main domains - Mental Health & Sexual Harrasment. Urja does have its main focus on these two issues but addresses all social issues, giving a platform to speak up, raise awareness and educate people regarding the same.

#Is there any stipend?
GirlUp is a campaign formed by enthusiastic volunteers. There are no fixed work hours or stipend; people come together to work voluntarily for the causes they believe in.

#Eligibility criteria, if any?
You have to be:
- Motivated towards the causes Urja works for.
- Deadline-oriented and dedicated while doing the required tasks.
- Creative and possess good communicative skills to contribute efficiently to Urja.

#Available domains of work?
There are six different domains working under Urja. You are welcome to be a part of as many as you like.
- Content Department
- Technical Department
- Outreach Department
- Social Media & Marketing Department
- Research Department
- PR Department
#Perks of joining a GirlUp chapter:
- Certificate: After the completion of the year as a member, you would get a verified certificate from the UN acknowledging the work you've done and the impact you've created.
- Networking & Exposure: Every GirlUp chapter requires members to interact with the diverse team members and third party organisations, corporates, NGOs, etc leading to increased networking and exposure.
- Awareness: While working for GirlUp Urja you will be required to research and read about gender related issues which would keep you informed and updated about several current issues, boosting your general knowledge.
- Getting to play a role in making the world a better place!

#Can I join multiple GirlUp chapters?
A person is not bound to work under one chapter, however we at GirlUp Urja believe that energy channelized in one direction is always more fruitful, not just for personal development but for the development of society.

#What's the working tenure duration of a GirlUp member?
When a person works for one year, they complete their member tenure. Based on their performance all throughout the year, council and cabinet for the next tenure are chosen.

#Is it only for girls?
Not al all. GirlUp chapters believe that in order to achieve gender equality, it is essential for all genders to be passionate towards working for the same cause. At GirlUp Urja, we welcome people of all genders wholeheartedly given that they are passionate and dedicated towards the cause.

Sexual harassment

The law defines sexual harassment as physical contact and advances, or a demand or request for sexual favors, or making sexually colored remarks, or showing pornography, or any other unwelcome physical, verbal, or non-verbal conduct of sexual nature. Any of these acts whether direct or implied, constitute sexual harassment under the law.

Sexual harassment is a risk in all facets of life: in shelter homes, in the workplace, in the home, on public transport. In the case of sexual assault, it was reported last year that 99 percent of cases go unreported. Police in some cities do not even register reports of sexual harassment, Reuters reported in 2016.

Mental Health Awareness

Mental health awareness has been steadily growing around the world, but it is still largely below what it should and could be. Often, getting treated for a mental illness is something looked at as “embarrassing” and people rarely want others to know it’s happening. Looking away from the issue doesn’t make the issue disappear–if anything, it gets worse.

Only about half of people in developed countries struggling with a mental illness get the help they need, while in developing countries 90 percent go without any form of treatment. The stigma against mental illness and lack of mental health awareness worsens already existing mental health issues.

With increased mental health awareness comes more support and care for those that need it. Currently, the public’s opinion on mental issues has a negative impact on those dealing with them, but the public has the power to make a positive impact. When people are educated on mental illnesses, their effects, and how often they occur, it lessens the bad rep they have. Mental health is just as important as physical health, in some aspects it’s more important because you can’t keep your physical health without a healthy mental capacity.

Campaign

Celebrating Womanhood - Live Session with Shrima Rai

A series of Instagram Live Sessions were organised by Girl Up Urja, as a part of our Woman's Day Campaign. The event concluded with the last session where we were joined by Mrs. Shrima Rai, who is a beauty, lifestyle and a fashion blogger and the cherry on top is that she also won the Mrs. India title in the year 2009.
She asserted the importance of feminism, the responsibilities of a mother, and also addressed the omnipresent stereotypes present in people's heads about working women. She also tried to shed light on the restrictions that are put on young girls in this insightful session.

How Covid-19 affects the Mental Health of Women - Live Session with Rimpa Sarkar

Our guest for the live session is Rimpa Sarkar. She is a Mental Wellness expert and holds the degree of Master’s in Clinical Psychology. She is a well known Cognitive Behavioral Therapist(CBT) and a Neuro Linguistic Programming(NLP) practitioner. To know more about her follow @rimpasarkar. In this most needed session the speaker talks about why mental wellness is necessary and also describes ‘How Covid affected our own mental health’. She also discussed how an individual can overcome the effects of covid on our mental health. We are very thankful to the speaker for giving her precious time to Girlup Urja.

Women Safety on Public Platforms & How Data is an Asset - Live session with Mysafetypin

For this live session we had Shreya V. Basu (Senior Program Associate @mysafetipin) as our notable speaker. She manages all the social media accounts of the organisation. In today's session we discussed ‘Women safety on public platforms and how personal data is an asset.’ The session started with a briefing about why women’s safety is necessary on the public handles. Also, the speaker elaborated on how to protect our privacy and data from being public. The session was very informative and also the need of the hour. We would like to give our heartiest thanks to @mysafetipin for such an interesting and informative session.

Cyberbullying and Grooming - Live Session with Rakshit Tandon

Our speaker for this insightful session was Mr Rakshit Tandon, a cybersecurity expert, advisor at Cyber Peace Foundation, consultant at IAMAI and Director of Hackershala. He has made lakhs of people aware about cyber related crimes.
Owing to the COVID-19 induced lockdown in India, people have the majority of their social and professional interactions on online mediums, they are more susceptible and vulnerable to comments and acts of hate. In such situations, both the bully and the victim are not aware of the repercussions and endangerment of their actions online.

Breaking Gender Stereotypes - Live Session with Shrima Rai

We were joined by the eminent speaker Ms. Shrima Rai who is a Beauty, Fashion and Lifestyle Blogger and also Miss India 2009.Playing with dolls is for girls while boys play with cars.
The session included discussion of many casual gender stereotypes like :- girls wear pink and boys should not like that color, girls should learn how to cook, girls are always reminded that they have to run a family and bear children when they grow into adults, they are taught unconsciously that certain roles are only meant for women like doing the daily household chores, etc. These are some of the stereotypes that are fed in the minds of children at their initial days of development.
